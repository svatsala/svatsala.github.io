Part 4B

For this part of the project, I drew inspiration from this paper which focuses on â€œMulti-Image Matching using Multi-Scale Oriented Patchesâ€ by Brown et al. The focus of part 4B is to automatically detect correspondences between the images and then warping the images as in part 4A, whereas part 4A focused on manually clicking correspondences and then warping the images. In order to do this, we must roughly detect corners in the images, spread them out a bit so we donâ€™t have concentrated points at corners, extract feature descriptor vectors from those points, conduct feature matching between the images using the descriptors, and finally use 4-point RANSAC to robustly estimate the homography matrix.

Harris Corners

Firstly, we use the Harris Interest Point Detector Algorithm to detect corners as shown below.

Adaptive Non-Maximal Suppression

Then, we use adaptive non-maximal suppression to only pick the points that are more spread out and have a higher likeliness of being a corner (the harris h-value). I accomplished this by going through the points, retaining the interest points around them have a higher h-value, finding the distance between each of the interest points and their respective center points, and then picking the first 250 points that have the highest distances from their centers. This greatly reduces the number of points weâ€™re dealing and ensures that our final set of correspondences are more distributed.

Extracting Feature Descriptors

For feature descriptor extraction, I blurred the images (to avoid aliasing), took a 40x40 pixel square around each of the points above, and resampled them down to 8x8 pixel squares. These 8x8 squares were then bias/gain normalized and ready for use!

Feature Matching & RANSAC

To match features, and thus feature descriptors, I went through each feature descriptor on image 1 and found the 2-NN on image 2 using sum of squared differences (SSD) as a metric. If the ratio between the first nearest neighbor and the second was less than some error parameter, then that feature detectorâ€™s first nearest neighbor was significantly closer to it than the second (a GOOD SIGN!). Therefore, I retained those points that fit the criteria and they are shown below!

In order to find a robust homography, I used 4-point RANSAC! This involved randomly selecting 4 pairs of these points, computing a homography matrix, multiplying it by the points, and calculating the number of inliers for 200 iterations. We retain the largest list of inliers and use that the compute the homography matrix estimate.
