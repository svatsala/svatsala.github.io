Light Field Camera

By: Shrestha Vatsala & Ethan Shedd

Inspired by: https://graphics.stanford.edu/papers/lfcamera/lfcamera-150dpi.pdf

Overview & Approach

In this project, we mimicked the depth refocusing and aperture adjustment effects of a camera from a computer. Given the Stanford Light Field Archive, we simply averaged certain images in several particular orders to create the effects below.

Depth Refocusing

To recreate a depth refocusing effect below, we shifted images by various scaled versions of their relative offsets and averaged them together. The scaling factor is known as alpha. It qualitatively dictates which part of the image we are focusing on and is derived by trial and error. Quantitatively, it scales the shifts in the images such that the average returns a blur in certain parts of the image and a focused area in other parts of the image.

Aperture Adjustment

Aperture dictates how large we want our radius of focus to be. The figure below shows, conceptually, how this works in a normal camera with a large aperture and a small aperture. To programmatically replicate a small aperture (a more focused image), we had to limit the number of images we decide to average because we want fewer samples such that there is less noise. Therefore, for a larger aperture, we want to use more samples to add more noise causing a blurring effect in the background.

Applications & Summary

Implementing light fields showed us how powerful simply averaging images can be when trying to replicate camera features like depth refocusing and aperture adjustments. We were also amazed that light fields could be used in a variety of applications like:

  • Virtual Reality & Augmented Reality
    • Light fields contribute to a more realistic rendering of VR/AR scenes because of their ability to capture and reproduce the direction of light rays. This enhances the sense of immersion.
    • Light fields enable the simulation of parallax and perspective shifts as users move within a virtual environment - improving the perception of depth and realism.
  • Medical Imaging
    • Light fields aid in capturing volumetric information, and this is beneficial in fields like radiology where 3D reconstructions of internal structures are required.
