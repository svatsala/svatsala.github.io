Computer Vision & Computational Photography

By: Shrestha Vatsala

Part 1: Defining Correspondences

Firstly, I defined correspondences between the two images of faces that I was morphing. I did this by building a point selection tool that allowed a one-to-one mapping of points between several images. Then, I created methods for averaging these two sets of correspondences and creating a Delaunay triangulation of the average set of points for part 2.

Part 2: Computing the ‚ÄúMid-way‚Äù Face

Computing the mid-way face between the above two images required calculating the average shape between the two images via the Delaunay Triangulation, warping the two faces into that shape, and averaging the colors together. The result is below:

Warping into the average triangulation (aka shape, geometry, etc.) required creating methods to compute the affine transformation matrix per triangle, create interpolation functions to get the appropriate color for the mid-way faces, and consequently dissolving those colors with weighted averages.

Part 3: The Morph Sequence

In order to create a morph sequence video, I added two parameters: the warp_frac and dissolve_frac. These specify the weight of each average when it comes to warping the shapes and dissolving the colors. This way, we can iterate and change these parameters over time, from 0 to 1, such that we start with image 1 and end with image 2. Part 4: The Mean Face of a Population

Utilizing this Danes dataset of Danish male and female subjects portraying varying expressions, I created the average face of the population. I decided to focus on the subpopulation of smiling female faces. Initially, I found the average face shape and triangulation of this subpopulation by averaging all of the x and y values of the corresponding points and deriving the Delaunay Triangulation. Afterwards, I morphed each image to this calculated shape. Some examples are shown here:

Sequentially, I averaged all of the traits of these warped images to create an image of the ‚Äúmean-face‚Äù of this subpopulation - mean in regards to shape and skin/eye/lip color.

For experimentation, I warped my face (shown in Image 4.1) to the average geometry I calculated earlier to produce Image 4.2. Additionally, I warped the image of the average face shown above to my facial geometry to produce Image 4.3.
